#### Excerpt set A — §What entity resolution is (backs Req 2.1–2.4)

- The guide opens by defining ER as the discipline that figures out when
  different records refer to the same entity, when they are related,
  and when they do not match at all. (Req 2.1)
- It lists entity types covered: person, organization, product, vessel,
  "among other entity types." Covers Req 2.2's people-and-organizations
  framing and broader type coverage.
- Framing of ER as underlying accurate counting appears explicitly:
  "is this one person or three? One company or five?" with the note
  that every downstream analytic depends on getting the count right.
  (Req 2.3)
- Discrimination framing: the guide states ER is not only about finding
  matches but equally about telling similar records apart; poor
  discrimination causes false positives (overmatching). (Req 2.4)
- Distinction from adjacent disciplines (identity and access management;
  identity authentication) is drawn but is out of scope for the Target
  File per Req 10.5.

#### Excerpt set B — §Why matching records is hard (backs Req 3.1–3.4)

- The guide illustrates the challenge with records that vary across
  name formats, address formats, and phone formats — covering Req 3.1's
  name variation, address change, and format inconsistency bullets.
- Data-entry errors (typos, transposed digits, abbreviations) are
  implicit in the "messy, incomplete, or contradictory" framing and in
  the "Rob Smith" vs "Robert Smith" hospital-chart example. (Req 3.1)
- False negative is described as missing a true match, which means
  treating one person as two, preventing a 360 view and losing critical
  context. Direct support for Req 3.2.
- False positive (overmatching) is illustrated with the Jr./Sr.
  single-letter difference and expanded as "juniors and seniors, twins,
  families with Patricks and Patricias under the same roof." Direct
  support for Req 3.3's father/son example.
- The guide states these edge cases are what simplistic matching
  systems get wrong — direct support for Req 3.4's claim that fuzzy
  matching alone cannot reliably distinguish these cases.

#### Excerpt set C — §How entity resolution works (backs Req 4.1–4.3)

The guide lays out the vendor-neutral pipeline as a five-step
enumeration that maps one-to-one onto Req 4.1:

1. Data ingestion and standardization — records collected from sources
   and mapped into a common format.
2. Candidate selection — grouping records using blocking / indexing to
   avoid all-pairs comparison.
3. Comparison and scoring — attribute-by-attribute similarity scoring
   (names, addresses, dates, identifiers).
4. Classification — pair-level match / no-match / possible-match
   decisions.
5. Entity clustering — matched records grouped into unified entities.

The guide then notes the advanced-engine behavior required by Req 4.3:
the most advanced ER systems compare each inbound record against
everything already known about an entity, rather than only pairwise
record-to-record, catching matches pairwise comparison would miss.

No code, no SDK references, no Senzing-specific calls appear in this
passage — clean fit for Req 4.2 (conceptual only).

#### Excerpt set D — §How Senzing handles it (backs Req 5.1–5.4 and Req 8.1–8.4)

Differentiators (Req 8) are clearly supported by the guide:

- Real-time / continuous resolution vs. batch-only processing is
  contrasted directly in the Traditional-ER-vs-Agentic-ER comparison
  table. Supports Req 8.1.
- "No training, no fine-tuning, no ER experts required" appears as one
  of the six non-negotiable capabilities and is repeated in the How Is
  Senzing Different section. Direct support for Req 8.2.
- Full attribution: "every record in the system should know exactly
  where it came from — source system and record ID, always"; with
  explicit framing that lossy processes like merge/purge or survivorship
  that discard information are wrong. Supports Req 8.3's attribution
  half.
- Full explainability: the guide names two required questions — why did
  these records match, and why did these records not match. Direct
  support for Req 8.3's explainability half.
- Scale: "Sub-second cold start" and "Scales from a laptop to billions
  of records." Supports Req 8.4.

Principle-based matching (Req 5.1–5.3) is **NOT** directly described in
the public guide. The guide supports the "no training, no fine-tuning,
no ER experts" posture (Req 5.1's negative half) and the preconfigured
posture (Req 5.4: "no training, no fine-tuning — minutes to onboard"
for a new data source), but the three attribute behaviors (frequency,
exclusivity, stability) and the phrase *principle-based matching* are
not on this page. These terms are in the pre-refresh Target_File and
must be preserved per Property 6 (non-regression). **Authoring note for
Task 2.5**: retain the three behaviors and their examples as-is from
the current file, flag every one of those sentences in the PR
description as "MCP re-verification pending" per Design §Error Handling.

#### Excerpt set E — §Relationships and ambiguous matches (backs Req 6.1–6.4)

- Relationship awareness: the guide states the best ER systems don't
  just match records; they track relationships between resolved
  entities, and the resulting entity-resolved graph is valuable in its
  own right. Supports Req 6.1.
- Disclosed vs discovered is defined explicitly: disclosed relationships
  are explicitly stated (CEO-of-Company example), discovered
  relationships are detected through shared attributes (common address
  or phone number). Direct support for Req 6.2.
- Ambiguous match is defined as a record that could legitimately belong
  to more than one entity. The George Foreman household example (a
  record with just name, home address, and home phone could be any of
  six people) is used. Supports Req 6.3's definition.
- Invisible false positive: the guide explicitly names these errors as
  undetectable until additional information reveals the mistake; most
  ER systems will arbitrarily assign such a record, creating a false
  positive that looks correct on inspection. Supports Req 6.3's
  consequence half.
- "Possible match" classification: the guide states a well-designed
  system flags the record as ambiguous rather than forcing a match,
  holding it in a "possible match" state until a distinguishing
  attribute arrives. Direct support for Req 6.4.

**Authoring tip**: the Target_File should paraphrase the Foreman
household setup without naming a specific individual; a generic "same
name, same household" phrasing satisfies Req 6.3 without importing
pop-culture specifics.

#### Excerpt set F — §What entity resolution produces (backs Req 7.1–7.4)

- Matched entities framed as a 360-degree customer view; the guide
  repeatedly uses "single, accurate view" and "360 view" for the
  golden-record output. Supports Req 7.1's matched-entities bullet and
  Req 7.2's golden-record framing.
- Cross-source relationships: the guide frames ER's output as a
  connected graph informing every downstream decision; case studies
  (GraphAware, Aptitude Global) illustrate entity-resolved graphs as
  the underpinning. Supports Req 7.1's cross-source-relationships
  bullet. The bootcamp's preferred vendor-in-procurement /
  supplier-in-ERP framing is not in the public guide but is consistent
  with it — treat that phrasing as editorial (carry-over from the
  pre-refresh Target_File per Property 6).
- Deduplication: the guide describes ER as removing duplicates within
  and across sources — "cleaner MDM, CRM, and other enterprise systems
  — fewer historical duplicates, and no new ones entering in real
  time." Supports Req 7.1's deduplication bullet.
- Use cases (Req 7.3): the guide's "Most Common Entity Resolution Use
  Cases" section explicitly lists fraud detection and prevention,
  investigations and law enforcement, customer 360 and marketing
  analytics, graph analytics and knowledge graphs, and risk and
  regulatory compliance. Any two of these (the bootcamp uses fraud
  detection, compliance/KYC, customer 360, investigations) are direct
  lifts.
- Business-value connection (Req 7.2, 7.4): the guide states ER is
  foundational infrastructure — "not a nice-to-have" — for AI and
  automation. The bootcamp-connecting sentence (Req 7.4) is editorial
  and does not need source backing.
